🏔 Rocky Mountains
Thrust upward 50–80M yrs ago by the Laramide Orogeny. Form the Continental Divide separating Atlantic and Pacific drainages. 3,000 mi long; highest point Mt. Elbert, CO at 14,440 ft.
⛰ Appalachians
Among Earth's oldest ranges (480M yrs). Once Himalayan-scale, erosion has worn them to rounded ridges averaging 3,000 ft. Run 1,500 mi from Alabama to Maine along the eastern seaboard.
🌊 Mississippi System
Drains 40% of the lower 48 — 1.2M sq mi of watershed. The Missouri (2,341 mi), Mississippi (2,340 mi) and Ohio together form North America's great inland waterway to the Gulf.
⚡ Tectonic Faults
Dashed red lines show: San Andreas Fault (CA), Cascadia Subduction Zone (Pacific NW), Wasatch Front (UT), and New Madrid Seismic Zone (MO/TN). These boundaries explain earthquake and volcanic activity.

🌲 Western Density
Southwest (CO, UT, AZ) and Pacific states show the highest trail concentration. The West holds 640M+ acres of federal land — the essential substrate for public trails. Density clusters follow park and forest boundaries.
🌾 Great Plains Gap
Kansas, Nebraska, and Iowa show sparse coverage. Vast agricultural ownership limits public access. Trail development correlates directly with publicly protected land — visible in both the mainland and Alaska.
📜 1968 Trails Act
Congress established the National Trails System. Today: the Appalachian Trail (2,193 mi), Pacific Crest (2,650 mi), and Continental Divide (3,100 mi) define the spine. Hawaii and Alaska add unique coastal and glacial routes.
🏝 Island Trails
Hawaii's 49 mapped trails concentrate on volcanic terrain (Big Island, Maui). Alaska's 29 trails cluster around Denali and Kenai Fjords — remote, high-difficulty routes in some of North America's most extreme wilderness.
